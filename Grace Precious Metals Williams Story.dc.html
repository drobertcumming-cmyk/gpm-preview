Bill’s story

Why I left the industry to build Grace.

An account from Bill Armour, Co-Founder & CEO

Two-minute version Read in full

The short version

In two minutes.

I am a pastor. I spent a decade in the Gold IRA industry before I helped found Grace. For most of those years I told myself I was an honest operator inside a difficult industry. I was selling a real product to people who needed help with their savings, and I was telling them the truth as far as the truth was visible to them on the call.

What I did not say was that the price they were paying on the way in was a fifth or a third more than they understood. What I did not say was that when they sold back to us, the buyback would not be referenced to what they had paid. What I did not say was that the conversations I was paid to have were structured by a compensation system designed to do exactly what it did.

I had a conversation with my own pastor that I had been avoiding. He asked me a question I had not asked myself. The answer kept me awake for a week.

I left. Andrew, my brother, and Duncan, who had reached his own version of the same reckoning from the marketing side of the industry, helped me build Grace. The company is what it is because of what each of us would not do again. The pricing is published. The buyback is at wholesale. The experts are salaried. The catalogue is bullion only. None of it is innovative. All of it is what should have been there from the beginning.

The full testimony · in writing

A decade inside.

I joined the Gold IRA industry the way most salespeople do — through a friend who told me the commissions were generous and the customers were grateful. He was right about the commissions. The gratitude was more complicated than I understood at the time.

For ten years I worked inside the industry, selling retirement-account rollovers into physical gold. The customers came in mostly through television and direct mail, responding to campaigns that spoke directly to their concerns about their savings — about the dollar, about the federal government, about the kind of country their grandchildren would inherit. They were almost always older. Almost always Christian. Almost always frightened, in some specific way, about what was happening to the money they had spent their lives setting aside.

My job was to call them back when they responded. To walk them through what a Gold IRA was. To answer their questions. To close them on a rollover. For the first few years I did the job the way it was set up to be done. I learned the scripts. I learned which objections came up most often and how to handle them. I got better at the close.

For most of those years I told myself I was an honest operator inside a difficult industry. The customers wanted gold. We sold them gold. The fact that the price they were paying was meaningfully higher than they understood it to be did not seem, to me, like the kind of thing that changed the moral picture. Markup is normal. Sales is normal. Margin is what businesses run on.

Gold IRA

A self-directed retirement account that holds physical gold or silver in an IRS-approved depository.

Bullion

Standard IRS-eligible coins and bars priced off the live spot price of the metal.

Numismatic

Coins sold above bullion value on the basis of rarity or marketing label. The category most associated with documented harm.

“The two numbers did not match. I am a pastor. I could not keep doing it.”

The conversation that changed my mind.

I had a conversation with my own pastor — not a customer, not someone I was trying to sell to, my own pastor — that I had been avoiding for a long time. I had been avoiding it because I knew what he was going to ask me, and I knew I did not have a good answer.

He did not ask the question I expected. He did not ask me whether what I was doing was legal. He did not ask me whether the customers were getting what they paid for. He asked me whether the customers, on the day they signed, knew the same things I knew.

I told him no. They did not. He asked me whether, if they had known what I knew, they would have made the same decision. I told him I did not know. Some of them, probably yes. Many of them, I thought, probably no. The price they were paying on the way in was a fifth or a third more than they understood. The buyback they would receive on the way out was not referenced to what they had paid — it was referenced to the bullion value of the metal at the day of sale, and on a product sold at a meaningful markup over bullion, the round-trip arithmetic was much harder than the entry conversation suggested.

He asked me whether the people I was selling to were the kind of people my church served on Sunday. I told him they were the same people. Older. Christian. Worried about their savings. Worried about the country. Often widows. Often the kind of people for whom the retirement account was the only meaningful asset they had.

He asked me how I had reconciled it. I told him the truth, which was that I had reconciled it by not asking the question he had just asked me.

Why this matters

The information asymmetry Bill names here is the structural feature behind the documented Gold IRA consumer-harm pattern. A buyer who does not know the same things the seller knows is buying half a transaction.

The week that followed.

I did not sleep well that week. I went to work. I made the calls. I closed two rollovers in those five days. The conversations were not different from any other week’s conversations. The customers were not different. I was different. I was hearing the questions my pastor had asked sitting on top of every script I had ever learned.

I called my brother on the Friday night. Andrew is a qualified accountant — a careful man, slow to anger, slower to act. I told him what my pastor had asked me. I told him what I had answered. I told him I did not know what to do.

Andrew is the kind of person who does not respond to a question of this size in the moment. He told me he would think about it. He called me back two days later with three pages of notes. The notes were not about whether I should leave. The notes were about what a Gold IRA company would look like if it was structured around the answers I should have been able to give my pastor. Published pricing. Buyback at wholesale. Salaried experts. Bullion only. He had worked through the arithmetic. He had concluded the model could sustain itself.

A few weeks later we started talking to Duncan. Duncan had spent his career on the marketing side of the same industry. He had not yet had my conversation with his pastor. By the time the three of us had been talking for a few months, he had had a version of it.

Andrew’s response

“Three pages of notes about what a Gold IRA company would look like if it was structured around the answers I should have been able to give my pastor.”

What I built instead.

Grace Precious Metals is what we built. None of what we built is innovative. The published spread is 11.1%, all in. The number is on the pricing page before any phone call. The buyback is at the wholesale price on the day, with no markdown. The catalogue is standard IRS-eligible bullion only — no numismatic, no exclusive, no premium, no proof, no labels. The experts are paid a salary, with no commission and no quotas. The Christian frame of the company is structural, not decorative — the principle the business is organised around is Proverbs 11:1, applied operationally rather than displayed for marketing.

The arithmetic is straightforward. A customer who rolls $150,000 into a Grace Gold IRA pays $16,650 in spread on the way in. There is no admin fee, no setup fee, no annual account fee. When that customer sells back, they receive the wholesale price on the day. The dealer’s revenue on the entire round trip is the spread on the entry — nothing more.

I want to say what I am most aware of about the customers I called for ten years. There was a particular conversation I had often. A widow, sometimes recently bereaved, sitting alone with a retirement account she had inherited or built and a fear about what was going to happen to it. The premium products had higher commissions than the bullion. The compensation system did what compensation systems do. Some of those women, when they later sold back, discovered that the round-trip arithmetic was not what they had understood it to be. The losses, totalled across the category, were measured in hundreds of millions of dollars over the years I was inside the industry.

I am not telling you this to claim I was the worst operator in the category. I was not. I was a normal operator inside a system structured the way it was structured. That is part of the point. The customers were not harmed by individuals failing to be honest. They were harmed by a system structured so that even the operators who thought they were honest were not telling the customer the things the customer needed to know.

Grace is built around the premise that the system is the problem. We changed the system. The pricing is published. The compensation is salaried. The catalogue is bullion. The buyback is at wholesale. We measure our weight, and we let the customer measure it too.

Proverbs 11:1 — the verse

“A false balance is an abomination to the Lord, but a just weight is His delight.”

The weight is what the customer is charged. The verse describes what the category, at its worst, has failed to do — and what we are structurally committed to doing differently. It is the verse above the door. It is also the answer to the question my pastor asked me on the day this began.
