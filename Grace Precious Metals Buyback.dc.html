Buyback mechanics

How buyback works — and why the exit number matters as much as the entry number.

Most buyers focus on the entry price. The exit price — what you receive when you sell back — is rarely part of the buying decision. For most customers, it is equally consequential.

The two numbers

Entry is the number you see. Exit is the number you don’t.

Entry — visible

When you purchase, the entry price is the number on the confirmation: spot, plus the wholesale or mint markup, plus the dealer’s markup. It appears on a document, so it feels concrete. You paid a price; you received an asset. The transaction is legible.

Exit — invisible

The exit price exists only in the future, at a moment you have not chosen, under conditions you cannot see. Most customers never ask about it — and most dealers never volunteer it. For the majority of holders, it is an unknown variable in what they believe is a known equation.

The equation

Entry markup + exit markdown = what the dealer actually extracted.

Anything quoted on just one side is half a number. The round-trip cost is the only honest measure.

Grace’s response

Buyback at wholesale, never below.

Grace buys back all IRS-eligible bullion at the prevailing wholesale price or higher at the time of transaction. No exit markdown, no liquidation fee, no processing charge, no minimum holding period. The price you receive on exit is the price Grace would pay a mint or wholesaler.

This is not a promotion. It is a structural commitment. If we charged 11.1% on the way in and extracted another 10% on the way out, the round-trip cost would be 21.1% — and the published number would be a lie.

What to ask

How to evaluate any company on buyback.

These two questions reveal the exit-side economics. Ask them before you fund. If the answers are vague, conditional, or unavailable in writing, that is itself an answer.

Ask in writing

A verbal commitment is not a commitment. If a dealer will not document buyback terms in a signed agreement before you fund, the terms are not binding.

01

“What is your buyback price relative to spot?”

The answer should be a specific number — ideally “at spot” or a stated percentage. If it is “competitive” or “market-based,” the markdown is discretionary and unpublished.

02

“Is there a minimum holding period before I can sell?”

Some dealers impose a 12–24 month period during which buyback is unavailable or penalized. This locks the customer in and removes competitive pressure on the exit price.
